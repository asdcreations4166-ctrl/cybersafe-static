Common Threats

These are the most common cybersecurity issues people face every day.

Safety Tips

Small habits make a big difference. Try these rules to keep your digital life protected.

Think Before You Click

Hover over links before you click, validate URLs, and be skeptical of urgent requests asking for credentials.

Update Often

Software updates patch security issues. Enable auto‑updates for your devices and apps.

Use 2FA

Two‑factor authentication adds an extra layer of protection even if a password is stolen.

Back Up Data

Regular backups protect you from ransomware and accidental loss. Keep copies off‑site or in the cloud.

Password Security

Learn best practices for creating and managing strong passwords.

Strong Passwords

  • Use unique passphrases of 12+ characters.
  • Include a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols.
  • Avoid common words and personal info.

Password Managers

A manager generates and stores complex passwords so you don’t have to memorize them. Choose reputable, audited tools.

Reuse Risks

Reusing passwords across sites means one breach can compromise multiple accounts. Change passwords after a leak.

Real‑Life Cases

These short scenarios highlight what can happen when security is ignored.

A finance team received an email appearing to be from the CEO asking for an urgent wire transfer. The email was spoofed, and the funds were moved to an attacker’s account before the fraud was discovered.

Tip: Always verify unusual requests via a second channel (call or in‑person) before sending money.

After a data breach, attackers used leaked credentials to log into the victim’s bank account and transfer funds. The password had been reused across multiple services.

Tip: Use a password manager and change compromised credentials immediately.

A user connected to an unencrypted coffee shop network and entered login details into a fake portal. The attacker captured the credentials and used them to access the user’s accounts.

Tip: Use a VPN and avoid logging into sensitive sites on public networks.

A small business opened an invoice attachment that contained ransomware. The network was encrypted, and attackers demanded payment to restore access.

Tip: Keep backups offline and never open attachments from unknown senders. Learn more.
